## Buyer Hypothesis

Primary buyer:

Founder-operator, technical founder, solo builder, or small team lead who has a serious software product idea but lacks a dependable path from rough idea to scoped work, product artifacts, app foundation, and execution queue.

Best-fit situations:

- The buyer has enough conviction to invest in clarity but is not ready to spend agency-MVP money.
- The buyer needs a product operating system more than a one-off prototype.
- The buyer wants traceable artifacts, not just a chat transcript or a pile of vague tasks.
- The buyer values local/customer-controlled source boundaries because the idea, repo, or internal process is sensitive.

Poor-fit situations:

- The buyer expects a production app from one short intake.
- The buyer needs enterprise security certification, compliance review, legal terms, or production deployment.
- The buyer only wants a cheap landing page.
- The buyer refuses any structured review gate and wants fully unattended automation.

## Primary Pain

The buyer is stuck between idea and execution.

They can describe what they want, but the path from product concept to buildable work is brittle:

- requirements are scattered across chat, notes, and repo context,
- agency discovery can be expensive and slow,
- AI coding can move fast but drift without a durable product map,
- implementation tickets appear before research, design, trust, or data boundaries are clear,
- useful context disappears between sessions.

## Buying Trigger

The strongest buying trigger is not "I need an MVP." It is:

```text
I have a product idea that I am serious about, and I need a governed path from idea to artifacts, app foundation, and next execution steps before I waste money or months.
```

Specific moments:

- preparing to hire a developer or agency,
- deciding whether an idea is worth a first build,
- trying to use AI coding tools without losing product direction,
- needing a pitchable product plan before spending on a full MVP,
- needing a private local workflow for a sensitive repo or idea.

## Offer Package

Working name:

```text
Launch Intake Managed Pilot
```

Buyer-readable promise:

```text
Bring one serious product idea. Leave with a traceable product packet, project KB, app-foundation or implementation handoff, risk notes, and a next-step roadmap.
```

Core deliverables:

1. Product Pipeline intake packet.
2. Research and comparative analysis brief.
3. Opportunity brief and first wedge.
4. Epic map and issue candidates with readiness gates.
5. Project KB or parent-KB update bundle.
6. AppBuilder invocation or app-foundation recommendation when approved.
7. Review notes covering risks, blockers, missing research, trust/IP concerns, and non-goals.
8. Next-lane roadmap and issue queue.

Optional add-on after the local runner contract exists:

- Run the handoff inside a customer-controlled local workspace and export only secret-free summary artifacts back to the project KB.

## Non-Promises

The offer must not promise:

- production deployment,
- legal/security certification,
- fully autonomous app creation,
- broad repo crawling,
- public SaaS access,
- fixed public pricing,
- full app completion from one intake.

## Pricing Hypothesis

Recommended test price:

```text
$4,500 fixed managed pilot for one product idea, one bounded artifact run, and one review-backed next-lane roadmap.
```

Price ladder to test privately:

| Package | Test price | When to use |
|---|---:|---|
| Founder Clarity Pilot | $2,500 | Idea-to-artifacts only; no code-aware handoff. |
| Launch Intake Managed Pilot | $4,500 | Recommended first offer; artifacts, KB, review, next-lane queue. |
| Local Workspace Pilot | $7,500-$12,500 | Adds customer-controlled runner/handoff once contract exists. |

Rationale:

- Current MVP market references cluster well above this for full builds, often in the tens of thousands.
- Discovery-only references commonly sit in low four figures, while four-week clarity/design sprints can run much higher.
- Nexa/Nodera should not price like a full agency MVP because v0 does not promise a production product.
- Nexa/Nodera should not price like a cheap template because the durable product-operating artifacts are the differentiated value.

Pricing rule:

Treat all numbers as private learning hypotheses until at least three buyer conversations confirm willingness to pay, objections, and scope expectations.

## Objections And Responses

| Objection | Response |
|---|---|
| "Will you build the app?" | Not as the first promise. The pilot creates the governed product and implementation substrate; app work happens only after the next lane is approved. |
| "Why not just use an AI coding tool?" | AI tools can write code quickly, but this offer preserves product reasoning, gates, KB memory, review artifacts, and source boundaries across sessions. |
| "Will you see my code?" | V0 defaults to local/customer-controlled execution for code-aware work. Customer source and secrets do not belong in hosted artifacts. |
| "Is this secure?" | Do not claim enterprise security. Claim a conservative data boundary: no secrets in KB/chat/docs; code-aware work stays local until a reviewed handoff says otherwise. |
| "Why not hire an agency?" | Agencies can be right for full MVPs. This pilot is a cheaper pre-build operating pass meant to reduce waste before agency-scale spend. |
| "What do I own?" | Customer-specific deliverables are for the customer. Nexa/Nodera retains reusable methods, templates, orchestration, component strategy, and platform IP. |
| "Can I get a fixed result?" | Fixed scope, yes. Fixed production outcome, no. The deliverable is a traceable decision and handoff package. |

## Validation Script

Use this script in founder/operator conversations:

1. "What have you already tried to turn this idea into a build plan?"
2. "Where did the process break down: product clarity, scope, design, technical plan, hiring, or trust?"
3. "Would a traceable packet, KB, app-handoff recommendation, and issue queue be useful before you spend on a full MVP?"
4. "What would make you nervous about letting an AI-assisted workflow near your idea or repo?"
5. "If the pilot cost $4,500 and delivered the artifacts in about a week, would you buy, pause, or reject? Why?"
6. "Would a $2,500 artifact-only version or a $7,500+ local-workspace version fit better?"

## Pass-Fail Criteria

Pass:

- At least three qualified buyers understand the offer without a long explanation.
- At least two say the $4,500 pilot is plausible if trust boundaries and sample artifacts are strong.
- Objections cluster around proof/examples rather than "I do not understand what this is."
- Buyers prefer artifact-backed clarity before a full MVP or agency build.

Fail:

- Buyers interpret the offer as a full MVP and feel disappointed by the scope.
- Buyers only value the code output and not the product-operating artifacts.
- Trust objections cannot be answered without building a much heavier security/compliance posture.
- The offer requires public SaaS, production deployment, or legal promises to be compelling.

## Recommendation

Proceed with the managed pilot offer as the next buyer-facing package, but keep it private and manually sold.

Do not build billing, public pricing, self-serve SaaS, or desktop packaging yet. The next two dependencies are:

1. Public trust/IP surface brief from Overwatch.
2. Local runner/customer-workspace handoff contract from Overwatch/AppBuilder.
